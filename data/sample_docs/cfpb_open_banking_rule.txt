CFPB Final Rule on Personal Financial Data Rights (Open Banking / Section 1033)

CONSUMER FINANCIAL PROTECTION BUREAU
12 CFR Part 1033
Final Rule: October 22, 2024

OVERVIEW

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) finalized its rule implementing Section 1033 of the Dodd-Frank Act, establishing for the first time a comprehensive framework for consumer access to personal financial data. Often referred to as the "Open Banking Rule," the regulation requires financial institutions to make consumer financial data available to consumers and their authorized third parties through standardized, developer-friendly interfaces.

The rule represents a fundamental shift in how financial data flows between institutions and aims to increase competition, lower switching costs for consumers, and enable innovation in financial services.

COVERED DATA PROVIDERS

The rule applies to the following data providers:
- Depository institutions (banks, credit unions, savings associations)
- Card issuers
- Non-depository institutions holding transaction accounts
- Digital wallet providers
- Any entity holding consumer financial data related to Regulation E or Regulation Z covered accounts

Exemptions:
- Depository institutions with total assets of $850 million or less (smaller institutions exempt)
- Entities not holding consumer transaction account data

COVERED DATA

Data providers must make the following consumer data available upon authorized request:

Transaction information:
- Amount, date, and description of transactions
- Pending and posted transactions
- Categorization data provided by the data provider

Account information:
- Account balance (current and available)
- Account number and routing number
- Account type and status
- Interest rate and credit limit (if applicable)
- Fees assessed
- Terms and conditions

Payment information:
- Upcoming bill amounts and due dates
- Auto-pay configurations
- Rewards program information (points, cashback balances)

NOT covered:
- Confidential commercial information
- Information collected for fraud prevention or compliance
- Information the data provider does not maintain in the ordinary course of business
- Algorithms, models, or proprietary analytics

AUTHORIZED THIRD PARTIES

Third parties seeking access to consumer data must:
1. Obtain the consumer's express informed consent through a clear authorization disclosure
2. Certify compliance with the rule's obligations
3. Limit data collection to what is reasonably necessary for the requested product or service
4. Delete data that is no longer necessary for the authorized purpose
5. Provide consumers with a mechanism to revoke access

Authorization Disclosure Requirements:
- Name and identity of the third party
- Description of the product or service for which data is being accessed
- Categories of data to be accessed
- Expected frequency of data access
- How long the authorization will last
- How the consumer can revoke authorization
- Contact information for the third party

DEVELOPER INTERFACE REQUIREMENTS

Data providers must make covered data available through a developer interface (API) that:
- Is machine-readable and standardized
- Provides data in a commercially reasonable timeframe
- Is available with commercially reasonable uptime
- Does not impose unreasonable access caps
- Allows third parties to access data without requiring consumers to share their login credentials (screen scraping prohibition)

Screen Scraping Prohibition:
The rule effectively bans credential-based screen scraping by requiring data providers to maintain developer interfaces. Third parties may not use consumer credentials to access data through consumer-facing interfaces.

Transition period: Data providers that do not yet have compliant developer interfaces must maintain existing data access methods until compliant interfaces are operational.

CONSUMER RIGHTS

Consumers have the right to:
- Access their financial data in machine-readable format
- Authorize third parties to access their data
- Revoke third-party access at any time
- Request deletion of their data from third parties
- Receive clear information about who has access to their data

Data portability: The rule is designed to reduce switching costs by enabling consumers to easily transfer their financial data to new providers, promoting competition among financial institutions.

DATA SECURITY REQUIREMENTS

Third parties accessing consumer data must:
- Maintain comprehensive data security programs
- Implement access controls and encryption
- Conduct regular security assessments
- Respond to and report security incidents
- Apply data minimization principles
- Retain data only as long as necessary for the authorized purpose

Data providers are responsible for:
- Authenticating third-party access requests
- Maintaining secure developer interfaces
- Monitoring for unauthorized access
- Denying access to third parties that fail to maintain proper authorization

STANDARD-SETTING BODIES

The rule recognizes the role of standard-setting bodies in developing technical standards for data sharing:
- Financial Data Exchange (FDX): Industry consortium developing the FDX API standard (currently FDX 6.0)
- CFPB may recognize industry standards through a formal process
- Recognized standards provide a "safe harbor" for compliance with certain technical requirements

IMPLEMENTATION TIMELINE

The rule has a phased compliance schedule based on entity size:

Tier 1 — Largest depository institutions ($250 billion+ total assets) and largest non-depository institutions:
- Compliance deadline: April 1, 2026

Tier 2 — Depository institutions ($10 billion to $250 billion total assets):
- Compliance deadline: April 1, 2027

Tier 3 — Depository institutions ($3 billion to $10 billion total assets):
- Compliance deadline: April 1, 2028

Tier 4 — Depository institutions ($1.5 billion to $3 billion total assets):
- Compliance deadline: April 1, 2029

Tier 5 — Depository institutions ($850 million to $1.5 billion total assets):
- Compliance deadline: April 1, 2030

IMPACT ON FINANCIAL SERVICES INDUSTRY

Banking:
- Banks must invest in API infrastructure and developer portals
- Potential loss of data advantage as switching costs decrease
- Opportunity to compete on service quality rather than switching friction
- Need to reevaluate data monetization strategies

Fintech:
- Reliable, standardized data access replaces fragile screen scraping
- Reduced dependency on data aggregators for basic connectivity
- Ability to build more sophisticated products with consistent data
- Compliance obligations as authorized third parties

Data Aggregators (Plaid, Yodlee, MX, Finicity):
- Fundamental business model shift from screen scraping to API-based access
- Evolution from pure data aggregation to value-added services
- Need to comply with third-party obligations under the rule
- Opportunity as intermediaries between data providers and fintechs

LEGAL CHALLENGES

Industry groups have filed legal challenges:
- Bank Policy Institute and Kentucky Bankers Association challenged the rule in November 2024
- Arguments include: CFPB exceeded statutory authority, rule imposes unreasonable compliance burdens, data security concerns
- The CFPB's funding structure (challenged in CFPB v. Community Financial Services Association) may affect the rule's durability

INTERNATIONAL CONTEXT

Open banking frameworks globally:
- UK: Open Banking Implementation Entity (OBIE) — operational since 2018
- EU: Payment Services Directive 2 (PSD2) — operational since 2019; PSD3 proposed
- Australia: Consumer Data Right (CDR) — operational since 2020
- Brazil: Open Finance — operational since 2021
- India: Account Aggregator framework — operational since 2021
- Canada: Consumer-Driven Banking framework — under development

The US rule is notable for its broader scope (beyond payments) and its emphasis on third-party obligations, reflecting lessons learned from earlier international implementations.
